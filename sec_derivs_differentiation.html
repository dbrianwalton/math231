Section 4.4 Differentiation

The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at a point \(x=a\) is the slope of the tangent line (linearization) of the function. In order to make sense of what that would mean, we needed to define the derivative as the limit of the slope of a secant line passing through the point \((a,f(a))\) and another point \((a+h, f(a+h))\) as the second point is chosen closer and closer to \(x=a\) by using \(h \to 0\text{.}\) Because this process could be done, in principle, at any point in the domain, this actually defines a function called the derivative function \(f'(x)\text{,}\) defined whenever this limit exists. In certain algebraic cases, we could allow a specific point \(a\) to be replaced by a generic variable \(x\) and still compute the limit.

The process described above gives the interpretation and justification for the derivative, but it is tedious as a process. Fortunately, there are patterns in the formulas for the derivative function in relation to the formulas for the original function. Rules describing these patterns establish a methodical process for calculating the derivative that does not require a limit. We will see that each of these rules is justified by the definition of the derivative in terms of limits and the use of limit rules that we learned previously.

Differentiation is the process of finding a derivative. This section focuses on how we differentiate formulas, the rules of derivatives, and how to think through the steps of identifying which rules apply in different circumstances.

Subsection 4.4.1 Derivative Functions and Polynomials

Polynomials are often used to illustrate that patterns occur in differentiation. Rather than go through the work by hand, we can let a computer do the algebra expansion and simplification and then look for patterns. One free tool that can do this work is SageMath. The cell below provides the steps that SageMath requires to find the derivative using the definition. Push the Evaluate button to find the result.

You should have found that for \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) the derivative is \(f'(x)=2x\text{.}\) Now, modify the cell so that \(f(x)=x^3\) and re-evaluate. Then try higher and higher powers. Can you see the pattern?

Mathematical notation is often created to reduce repetitive writing. It seems tedious to need to name our function \(f(x)\) each time we change the formula so that we can talk about its derivative \(f'(x)\text{.}\) We introduce the derivative operator \(\frac{d}{dx}\) so that we can focus on the formula.

An operator is like a function in that it has an input and an output. Instead of taking values as the input, an operator takes an expression or function. The derivative operator takes a function as its input and gives the derivative function as the output. Our earlier work showed that for \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) we have \(f'(x)=2x\text{.}\) That is, we think of \(x^2\) as the input of the step and \(2x\) as the output, and write

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2] = 2x\text{.} \end{equation*}

\(f(x)=x^5\) \(f'(x)=5x^4\) \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^5] = 5x^4\)

Can you see the pattern emerging? We want to summarize this pattern as a rule, which we will call the power rule.

Theorem 4.4.2. Power Rule for Derivatives.

For a function of the form \(f(x)=x^n\text{,}\) where \(n\) is a constant, we have \(f'(x)=nx^{n-1}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

At the moment, we only have looked at a few examples where this pattern holds. If we were the first to notice the pattern, we would call this a conjecture until we could prove that our pattern really worked in all cases. Fortunately, it actually works for any real number as long as the value is defined. (Some powers of negative numbers don't make sense.)

What other patterns can we discover? Well, polynomials are formed by adding different powers together and possibly include multiples of powers. So let's use SageMath to explore examples like that.

Polynomials, when written as a sum of constants times simple powers, have a very simple pattern for finding their derivatives. We create a new polynomial for the derivative. We get the same number of terms as the original function, but with terms changing as follows:

  • The constant multiple is multiplied by the original power.

  • The power on each term goes down by one.

  • A linear term in the original polynomial of the form \(ax+b\) results in a constant term in the derivative polynomial of the slope \(a\text{.}\)

Example 4.4.3.

Find the following derivatives.

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 4x^5 + 3x^4 - 7x^2 + 5x - 1\big]\)

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\big[ (3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7)\big]\)

Solution.

In the first exercise, our function is \(f(x)=4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1\text{.}\) This polynomial is written as a sum of terms that are constants times powers \(4x^5\text{,}\) \(3x^4\text{,}\) \(-7x^2\text{,}\) and a linear term \(5x-1\text{.}\) This is exactly the form for which our differentiation process can address.

The derivatives of the powers will be the same constant multiples times the original power times \(x\) raised to a power decreased by one. The derivative of the linear term \(5x-1\) will be the slope \(5\text{.}\) Putting this together, we write:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\big[4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1] &= 4 \cdot 5x^4 + 3 \cdot 4x^3 - 7 \cdot 2x + 5\\ &= 20x^4 +12x^3 - 14x + 5 \end{align*}

That is, the function \(f(x)=4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1\) has a derivative function \(20x^4+12x^3-14x+5\text{.}\)

In the second exercise, our function is \(f(x)=(3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7)\text{.}\) We need to be paying attention to the structure of the formula. It is written as a product, so our differentiation process is not designed to address the function in this form. We will need to multiply it out (distribute) in order to rewrite the formula so that it is a sum of terms.

\begin{align*} f(x) &= (3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7)\\ &= 4x(3x^2+2x-5) + 7(3x^2+2x-5)\\ &= 12x^3 + 8x^2 -20x + 21 x^2 + 14x - 35\\ &= 12x^3 +29 x^2 - 6x - 35 \end{align*}

Now that we have rewritten our function, see that it is a sum of constant multiples of powers \(12x^3\text{,}\) \(29x^2\text{,}\) and a linear term \(-6x-35\text{.}\) Using our polynomial differentatiation rule:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\big[(3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7) &= \frac{d}{dx}\big[12x^3+29x^2-6x-35\big]\\ &= 12 \cdot 3x^2 + 29 \cdot 2x + -6\\ &= 36 x^2 + 58 x - 6 \end{align*}

There is an important observation to make about the notation in the set of equations above. When we rewrote the formula of \(f(x)\) from being a product to being a sum, we did not yet use differentiation. So in the notation, you should see that we left the derivative operator alone but changed the formula that appeared as the input. When we actually used differentiation to find derivative terms, we stopped writing \(\frac{d}{dx}\text{.}\) These equations are saying in essence, “The derivative of one formula that is a product is equal to the derivative of a rewritten formula that is now a sum and both are equal to a new polynomial given by a particular new formula”. We are not saying that the original function is equal to the final formula that represents the derivative.

Because the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}\) is true (we've not yet shown this) for any constant power \(n\text{,}\) the same process we used for polynomials to find a derivative will also work for any function that is written as a sum of constants times elementary powers of \(x\text{.}\) Polynomials require that the powers are nonnegative integers. When we have a function that is a sum of term with at least one that is a power other than a nonnegative integer (either a negative integer or a rational number), we will call our function a generalized polynomial.

One of our learning outcomes is associated with differentiating generalized polynomials. You should expect that the first step will be to rewrite the formula as a sum so that our procedure is valid. We will soon learn additional derivative rules that allow us to calculate a derivative without this step (product and quotient rules), but this learning outcome focuses on using the generalized polynomial approach.

Example 4.4.4. Learning Objective D-9.

Find the following derivatives without using the product or quotient rules.

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\Big[ \frac{2x^5 - x^3 + 7x - 5}{4x^2}\Big]\)

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 5\sqrt{x} (x^2 + 2x - 5)\big]\)

Solution.

The first example looks at a function that is originally written as a quotient. Because the denominator is a simple power, we can rewrite this in the form of a generalized polynomial.

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{2x^5 - x^3 + 7x - 5}{4x^2} \right] &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{2x^5}{4x^2} - \frac{x^3}{4x^2} + \frac{7x}{4x^2} - \frac{5}{4x^2} \right]\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{1}{2} x^3 - \frac{1}{4} x + \frac{7}{4} x^{-1} - \frac{5}{4} x^{-2} \right]\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot 3x^2 - \frac{1}{4} + \frac{7}{4} \cdot -1x^{-2} - \frac{5}{4} \cdot -2x^{-3}\\ &= \frac{3}{2} x^2 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{7}{4x^2} + \frac{5}{2x^3} \end{align*}

Note that for negative powers, when the power drops by 1, it moves away from zero. A common mistake is to reduce the size of the power rather than literally subtracting 1, which is what is required.

The second example looks at a function that is originally written as a product. In addition, the function involves a square root. To proceed with this problem, we need to recognize that a square root corresponds to a power of \(\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) In addition, we need to multiply out the terms in order to have a formula in the standard representation.

Our first step is to rewrite the original function so that the process of differentiation of generalized polynomials applies (first three lines). Then we can apply the power rule to each term.

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 5\sqrt{x} (x^2 + 2x - 5)\big] & = \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 5 x^{1/2}(x^2 + 2x - 5) \big]\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 5x^{5/2} + 10 x^{3/2} - 25 x^{1/2} \big]\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\big[ 5x^{5/2} + 10 x^{3/2} - 25 x^{1/2} \big]\\ &= 5 \cdot \frac{5}{2}x^{3/2} + 10 \cdot \frac{3}{2} x^{1/2} - 25 \cdot \frac{1}{2} x^{-1/2}\\ &= \frac{25}{2}x^{3/2} + 15 x^{1/2} - \frac{25}{2} x^{-1/2} \end{align*}

Subsection 4.4.2 Derivative Rules

The definition of the derivative is in terms of limits. Limits have rules based on the structure of the function. These limit rules allow us to create derivative rules that are similarly based on the structure of the function's formula.

Derivative Rules.

Suppose that a function is constructed from simpler functions, each of which has a known derivative or differentiation rule. In particular, we will assume that \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) stand for differentiable functions and that \(k\) stands for some constant.

\begin{align*} & \frac{d}{dx}\big[f(x) + k\big] = f'(x) & \text{Constant Sum Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\big[k \cdot f(x)\big] = k \cdot f'(x) & \text{Constant Multiple Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\big[ f(x) + g(x) \big] = f'(x) + g'(x) & \text{Sum Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\big[ f(x) - g(x) \big] = f'(x) - g'(x) & \text{Difference Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\big[ f(x) \cdot g(x) \big] = f'(x) g(x) + f(x) g'(x) & \text{Prodcut Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \right] = \frac{f'(x) g(x) - f(x) g'(x)}{(g(x))^2} & \text{Quotient Rule}\\ & \frac{d}{dx}\left[ f \circ g(x) \right] = \frac{d}{dx}\left[ f\big(g(x)\big) \right] = f'\big( g(x) \big) \cdot g'(x) & \text{Chain Rule} \end{align*}

In many instances, doing calculus is code for using these rules. The differentiation of polynomials that we explored earlier was an example of using the constant multiple rule along with the sum rule on individual terms that were powers. The power functions were the elementary functions whose derivatives were known. These rules for constructed functions justify why we can treat terms separately.

We have multiple learning objectives relating to the proper use of these rules.

  • Learning Objective D-10: Using the quotient rule to find a derivative of a rational function.

  • Learning Objective D-11: Using the chain rule to find a derivative for a composition involving a power.

  • Learning Objective D-12: Using all of the derivative rules in an abstract setting.

Example 4.4.5. Learning Objective D-10.

For \(\displaystyle R(x) = \frac{x^2+2x}{2x^2+1}\text{,}\) find all points where the graph has horizontal tangents.

Solution.

A horizontal tangent corresponds to a slope that is zero. We need to find the derivative function \(R'(x)\) and determine values so that \(R'(x)=0\text{.}\) The function \(R(x)\) is a rational function because it is a quotient of two polynomials.

To find \(R'(x)\text{,}\) we need to use the quotient rule because \(R(x)\) itself is a quotient.

\begin{align*} R'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{x^2+2x}{2x^2+1} \\ &= \frac{\frac{d}{dx}[x^2+2x] \cdot (2x^2+1) - (x^2+2x) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[2x^2+1]}{(2x^2+1)^2} \\ &= \frac{(2x+2)(2x^2+1) - (x^2+2x)(4x)}{(2x^2+1)^2} \\ &= \frac{(4x^3+4x^2+2x+2) - (4x^3+8x^2)}{(2x^2+1)^2} \\ &= \frac{4x^3+4x^2+2x+2 - 4x^3 - 8x^2}{(2x^2+1)^2} \\ &= \frac{-4x^2+2x+2}{(2x^2+1)^2} \end{align*}

Experience has shown me that we always want to rewrite the numerator in hopes of factoring in the future, but almost never want to rewrite the denominator since it is already factored.

Once we know \(R'(x)\text{,}\) we create and solve the equation \(R'(x)=0\text{.}\) Because \(R'(x) = \frac{-4x^2+2x+2}{(2x^2+1)^2}\) is itself a quotient, \(R'(x)= 0\) is solved by determining where the numerator equals zero. In this case, we can factor that resulting polynomial.

\begin{gather*} R'(x) = 0\\ \frac{-4x^2+2x+2}{(2x^2+1)^2} = 0\\ -4x^2+2x+2 = 0\\ -2(2x^2-x-1) = 0\\ -2(2x+1)(x-1) = 0 \end{gather*}

So \(R'(x)=0\) at \(x=-\frac{1}{2}\) and at \(x=1\text{.}\) We can verify whether these values make sense by looking at a graph of \(R(x)\text{.}\)

Example 4.4.7. Learning Objective D-11.

Find \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{2}{5(x^2+3x)^6}\right]\text{.}\)

Solution.

As written, this function is a quotient. We can rewrite it as a composition of a power by rewriting the power in the denominator as multiplication by a negative power.

\begin{align*} f(x) &= \frac{2}{5(x^2+3x)^6}\\ &= \frac{2}{5} (x^2+3x)^{-6} \end{align*}

Our function \(y=f(x)\) is now the composition (or chain)

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y = \frac{2}{5} u^{-6} \\ u = x^2+3x \end{cases} \end{equation*}

We can compute the derivatives of each stage of the composition:

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \frac{dy}{du} = \frac{2}{5} \cdot -6 u^{-7} = \frac{-12}{5} u^{-7}\\ \frac{du}{dx} = 2x+3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

The chain rule says that the derivative of a composition (chain) is the product of the derivatives of each stage:

\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{-12}{5} u^{-7} \cdot (2x+3) \end{equation*}

We finish by substituting back in the formula for \(u\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{-12}{5(x^2+3x}^7} \cdot (2x+3) = \frac{-12(2x+3)}{5(x^2+3x)^7}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Example 4.4.8. Learning Objective D-12.

Given \(\displaystyle h(x) = 2x f(x^2) - \frac{1}{g^3(x)}\text{,}\) find \(h'(x)\) and then calculate \(h'(2)\) using the data in the table.

\(x\) \(f(x)\) \(f'(x)\) \(g(x)\) \(g'(x)\)
2 4 -2 -3 2
4 1 2 4 -2
8 3 -1 2 -3

Solution.

The idea behind this learning objective exercise is for you to recognize the structure of the formula and then use the appropriate derivative rules based on that structure. The functions \(f\) and \(g\) are placeholders for functions that we know derivative values so that we don't rely on just the power rule as the example application.

The overall function is a difference of two formulas, \(2x f(x^2)\) and \(\frac{1}{g^3(x)}\text{.}\) The first of these is a product of \(2x\) and \(f(x^2)\text{.}\) We know how to find the derivative of \(2x\) (a linear function) and \(f(x^2)\) is a composition of \(f\) with an inner function \(x^2\text{.}\) The second term can be rewritten as \((g(x))^-3\text{,}\) which is itself now a composition of \(u^{-3}\) with \(u=g(x)\text{.}\)

When we use the derivative rules for a difference, we just subtract the derivatives of the terms separately. The product will require the product rule. We work through the rules in turn to find the final answer.

\begin{align*} h'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ 2x f(x^2) - \frac{1}{g^3(x)} \right]\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ 2x f(x^2) \right] - \frac{d}{dx}\left[\left(g(x)\right)^{-3} \right]\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\left[ 2x \right] \cdot f(x^2) + 2x \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\left[ f(x^2) \right] - \frac{d}{dx}\left[\left(g(x)\right)^{-3} \right]\\ &= 2 \cdot f(x^2) + 2x \cdot f'(x^2) \cdot (2x) - \frac{d}{dx}\left[\left(g(x)\right)^{-3} \right]\\ &= 2 \cdot f(x^2) + 2x \cdot f'(x^2) \cdot (2x) - (-3u^{-4}) \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \Big|_{u=g(x)}\\ &= 2 \cdot f(x^2) + 4x^2 \cdot f'(x^2) + 3(g(x))^{-4} \cdot g'(x)\\ &= 2 \cdot f(x^2) + 4x^2 \cdot f'(x^2) + \frac{3g'(x)}{(g(x))^{4}} \end{align*}

Now that we have a formula for \(h'(x)\text{,}\) we can calculate \(h'(2)\) using the provided information. First, we just substitute \(x=2\) into the formula:

\begin{equation*} h'(2) = 2 \cdot f(2^2) + 4\cdot 2^2 \cdot f'(2^2) + \frac{3g'(2)}{(g(2))^{4}} \end{equation*}

Then we find the values of these terms:

\begin{align*} h'(2) &= 2 f(4) + 16 f'(4) + \frac{3g'(2)}{(g(2))^4}\\ &= 2 \cdot 1 + 16 \cdot 2 + \frac{3\cdot 2}{(-3)^4\\ &= 2 + 32 + \frac{6}{81}\\ &= 34 + \frac{2}{27} \end{align*}
