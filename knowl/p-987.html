Notice that no formulas or variables are directly introduced. Identifying these as appropriate will be part of solving the problem.

  • Identify the objective.

    We look for a phrase suggesting what needs to be as large or as small as possible. In this problem, we see “makes the surface area as small as possible”. This means our objective is the surface area of the rectangular box.

  • Identify the control variables.

    How do we compute surface area? A rectangular box has six sides. Because our box has a square base, there will be two dimensional lengths—the length of each side of the square base and the height of the box. We need to state our variables:

    \begin{align*} x &= \text{length of a side of the square base}\\ y &= \text{height of the box} \end{align*}

  • Write a formula for the objective.

    The top and bottom sides are both square with area \(x^2\text{.}\) The other four sides are all the same size with base \(x\) and height \(y\) with area \(xy\text{.}\) Our objective will be the total area \(A\) calculated by

    \begin{equation*} A = 2x^2 + 4xy\text{.} \end{equation*}

    This is a function of two control variables.

  • Identify constraints.

    In this problem, we are told that the box must contain a volume of \(2 \, \text{m}^3\text{.}\) This is a constraint. The formula for volume of our box will be \(V = x^2 y\text{,}\) and our constraint equation becomes

    \begin{equation*} x^2 y = 2\text{.} \end{equation*}

  • Solve the constraint equation.

    We want to solve the constraint equation so that one control variable is a function of the other. In this problem, it will be easier to solve for \(y\) as a function of \(x\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{2}{x^2} = 2x^{-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  • Rewrite the objective as a function of one variable.

    We replace \(y\) with the formula \(y=2x^{-2}\) in our objective formula to get \(A\) written as a function of \(x\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} A(x) = 2x^2 + 4x \cdot 2x^{-2} = 2x^2 + 8x^{-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  • Identify the domain and solve the corresponding global extreme problem.

    There were no extra constraints about the values of the variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) There is a physical constraint that the values can not be negative. Our formula for \(y\) has a domain constraint that \(x \ne 0\text{,}\) so we have a domain \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)

    This domain is not a closed interval, so global extreme values are not guaranteed. We will need to consider limits \(x \to 0\) and \(x \to \infty\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} \lim_{x \to 0^+} A(x) &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} [2x^2 + 8x^{-1}]\\ &= 0 + \infty = +\infty\\ \lim_{x \to \infty} A(x) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} [2x^2 + 8x^{-1}]\\ &= \infty + 0 = \infty \end{align*}

    We can see that there will be no global maximum for the surface area. But we will be able to find a global minimum.

    We find critical points by solving \(A'(x) = 0\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} A'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}[2x^2 + 8x^{-1}]\\ &= 4x - 8x^{-2} = 4x - \frac{8}{x^2}\\ &= \frac{4x^3-8}{x^2} \end{align*}

    The critical points will be solutions of \(4x^3-8 = 0\) or \(x^3=2\text{.}\) The only critical point is at \(x=\sqrt[3]{2}\text{.}\) Because \(A''(x) = 4+16x^{-3}\) has \(A''(\sqrt[3]{2}) = 12\) is positive, we know that \(A\) has a local minimum at this point. Because there are no other critical points and we already eliminated both ends of the interval as being unbounded positive values, the box with minimum surface area will be constructed with \(x = \sqrt[3]{2}\text{.}\)

  • Find the values of all control variables.

    We found the value of \(x=\sqrt[3]{2}\text{.}\) We also need the value of \(y\text{.}\) From the constraint, we found \(y=2x^{-2}\) so that we can find

    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{2}{(\sqrt[3]{2})^2} = \frac{2}{2^{2/3}} = 2^{1/3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    That is, we find \(y=x=\sqrt[3]{2}\text{,}\) and our rectangular box is actualy a cube.
