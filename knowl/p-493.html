What does this actually mean? Let us focus on \(x \to \infty\text{.}\) We need to imagine a sequence of \(x\)-values that are unbounded and increasing, \(x_1 \lt x_2 \lt x_3 \lt \cdots\) with \(x \to +\infty\text{.}\) We look at the values of the function \(f(x_1), f(x_2), f(x_3), \ldots\text{.}\) If this sequence always converges to \(L\text{,}\) \(f(x) \to L\text{,}\) no matter which unbounded sequence is chosen, then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\text{.}\)
