Solution 4.4.3.1.

In the first exercise, our function is \(f(x)=4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1\text{.}\) This polynomial is written as a sum of terms that are constants times powers \(4x^5\text{,}\) \(3x^4\text{,}\) \(-7x^2\text{,}\) and a linear term \(5x-1\text{.}\) This is exactly the form for which our differentiation process can address.

The derivatives of the powers will be the same constant multiples times the original power times \(x\) raised to a power decreased by one. The derivative of the linear term \(5x-1\) will be the slope \(5\text{.}\) Putting this together, we write:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\big[4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1] &= 4 \cdot 5x^4 + 3 \cdot 4x^3 - 7 \cdot 2x + 5\\ &= 20x^4 +12x^3 - 14x + 5 \end{align*}

That is, the function \(f(x)=4x^5+3x^4-7x^2+5x-1\) has a derivative function \(20x^4+12x^3-14x+5\text{.}\)

In the second exercise, our function is \(f(x)=(3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7)\text{.}\) We need to be paying attention to the structure of the formula. It is written as a product, so our differentiation process is not designed to address the function in this form. We will need to multiply it out (distribute) in order to rewrite the formula so that it is a sum of terms.

\begin{align*} f(x) &= (3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7)\\ &= 4x(3x^2+2x-5) + 7(3x^2+2x-5)\\ &= 12x^3 + 8x^2 -20x + 21 x^2 + 14x - 35\\ &= 12x^3 +29 x^2 - 6x - 35 \end{align*}

Now that we have rewritten our function, see that it is a sum of constant multiples of powers \(12x^3\text{,}\) \(29x^2\text{,}\) and a linear term \(-6x-35\text{.}\) Using our polynomial differentatiation rule:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\big[(3x^2+2x-5)(4x+7) &= \frac{d}{dx}\big[12x^3+29x^2-6x-35\big]\\ &= 12 \cdot 3x^2 + 29 \cdot 2x + -6\\ &= 36 x^2 + 58 x - 6 \end{align*}

There is an important observation to make about the notation in the set of equations above. When we rewrote the formula of \(f(x)\) from being a product to being a sum, we did not yet use differentiation. So in the notation, you should see that we left the derivative operator alone but changed the formula that appeared as the input. When we actually used differentiation to find derivative terms, we stopped writing \(\frac{d}{dx}\text{.}\) These equations are saying in essence, “The derivative of one formula that is a product is equal to the derivative of a rewritten formula that is now a sum and both are equal to a new polynomial given by a particular new formula”. We are not saying that the original function is equal to the final formula that represents the derivative.
