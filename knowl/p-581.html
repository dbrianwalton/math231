See if you can deconstruct the formula on the right on your own. You should then be able to follow the steps of limit justification shown below. Notice that multiplication by 2 is a constant multiple and adding the constant 1 to \(x^2\) is a constant sum, allowing us to avoid needing to find limits of the constants on their own.

Step Limit Justification
1 \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{f(x)}{x-3} = 5\) Given
2 \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} x = 3\) Limit of Identity
3 \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} x+2 = 5\) Limit of a Linear Function
4
\begin{align*} \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} x^2 &= \lim_{x \to 3} x \cdot \lim_{x \to 3} x\\ &= 3 \cdot 3 = 9 \end{align*}
Limit of a Product
5
\begin{align*} \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} x^2+1 &= \left(\lim_{x \to 3} x^2 \right) + 1\\ &= 9+1 = 10 \end{align*}
Limit of a Constant Sum
6
\begin{align*} \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} 2(x^2+1) &= 2 \cdot \left(\lim_{x \to 3} x^2+1 \right)\\ &= 2 \cdot 10 = 20 \end{align*}
Limit of a Constant Multiple
7
\begin{align*} \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{2(x^2+1)}{x+2} &= \frac{20}{5} = 4 \end{align*}
Limit of a Quotient
8
