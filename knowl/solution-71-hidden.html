For application problems, one of the key challenges is simply interpreting the question. In this problem, we are asked a question about how fast, which should be interpreted as finding a rate of change. Specifically, we want to find how fast the raindrop is losing water. How do we measure how much water is in a raindrop? That will be a volume of water. So we are being asked to find the rate of change of the volume of the raindrop. If we write \(V\) for volume and \(t\) for time, we need to find \(\frac{dV}{dt}\text{,}\) the instantaneous rate of change of volume with respect to time.

A second challenge of application problems is interpreting what information we are given. We are told a value of the radius (in millimeters) and how fast it is changing (in millimeters per second). We need a variable for the radius, say \(r\text{.}\) The given information is the value \(r=0.8\) mm. When we are given a rate of change, that is a derivative, in this case \(\frac{dr}{dt} = -0.05\) mm/s. The value is negative because we know the radius is decreasing.

Once we identify what information is given and what information is desired, we identify equations relating the quantities. From geometry, we start with the equation relating volume \(V\) and radius \(r\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3\text{.} \end{equation*}

In this equation, both \(V\) and \(r\) need to be considered as functions of time \(t\text{,}\) which does not directly appear in the geometric equation. Using function notation, the equation would be rewritten

\begin{equation*} V(t) = \frac{4}{3} \pi (r(t))^3\text{.} \end{equation*}

To find the related rates equation, we need to compute the derivative with respect to time. Because the radius function is raised to a power, the derivative will require the chain rule.

\begin{align*} \frac{dV}{dt} = V'(t) &= \frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{4}{3} \pi (r(t))^3 \right]\\ &= \frac{4}{3} \pi \frac{d}{dr}[r^3] \cdot \frac{d}{dt}[r(t)]\\ &= \frac{4}{3} \pi \cdot 3 r^2 \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}\\ &= 4 \pi \cdot r^2 \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} \end{align*}

In the chain rule, the derivative of the outside was \(\frac{dV}{dr}\) while the derivative of the inside was \(\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\)

Now that we have the related rates equation, we use the known values to solve for the unknown value. We know at the instant in question \(r=0.8\) mm and \(\frac{dr}{dt}=-0.05\) mm/s. We substitute these values into the equation to get

\begin{equation*} \frac{dV}{dt} = 4 \pi (0.8 \, \text{mm})^2 \cdot (-0.05 \, \text{mm/s}) = -0.128 \pi \, \text{mm}^3/\text{s} \approx -0.4021 \, \text{mm}^3/\text{s}\text{.} \end{equation*}

A negative rate of change occurs because the volume is also decreasing as time passes.
