We find the related rates equation by creating a new equation about the rates of change. With two different sides to the equation for the squared distance, we will calculated the derivative of both sides of the equation and they must be equal to one another. Remember that all of our variables \(A, B, C\) are actually functions of time. On the left, we have the derivative of the square of \(C\text{,}\) which will require a chain rule (outside \(C^2\text{,}\) inside \(C(t)\)),

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}[\left(C(t)\right)^2] = \frac{d}{dC}[C^2] \cdot \frac{d}{dt}[C(t)] = 2C \cdot \frac{dC}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the right, we have the derivative of the sum of the squares of \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) which will again require the chain rule on each square,

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}[\left(A(t)\right)^2 + \left(B(t)\right)^2] &= \frac{d}{dt}[\left(A(t)\right)^2] + \frac{d}{dt}[\left(B(t)\right)^2]\\ &= \frac{d}{dA}[A^2] \cdot \frac{d}{dt}[A(t)] + \frac{d}{dB}[B^2] \cdot \frac{d}{dt}[B(t)]\\ &= 2A \cdot \frac{dA}{dt} + 2B \cdot \frac{dB}{dt} \end{align*}

Consequently, the related rates equation is

\begin{equation*} 2C \cdot \frac{dC}{dt} = 2A \cdot \frac{dA}{dt} + 2B \cdot \frac{dB}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
