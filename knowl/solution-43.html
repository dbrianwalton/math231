Solution 3.4.6.1.

We need to compute limits of \(Q(x)\) as \(x \to \pm \infty\text{.}\) In both cases, we will want to use a revised formula that expresses \(Q(x)\) as a product of terms rather than quotient of sums. The dominant power in the numerator is \(x^5\) and the dominant power in the denominator is \(x^2\text{.}\) We factor each of those powers out of their respective expressions. For \(x \ne 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} Q(x) &= \frac{2x^5-3x^2-7}{x^2+2x-5} \\ &= \frac{x^5 \cdot (2 - \frac{3}{x^3} - \frac{7}{x^5}}{x^2 \cdot (1 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{5}{x^2})} \end{align*}

Next we rewrite this as a product, grouping the ratio \(\frac{x^5}{x^2}\) in order to simplify it:

\begin{align*} Q(x) &= \frac{x^5}{x^2} \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{x^3} - \frac{7}{x^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{5}{x^2}}\\ &= x^3 \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{x^3} - \frac{7}{x^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{5}{x^2}} \end{align*}

End behavior is characterized by the limits \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \pm \infty} Q(x)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \lim_{x \to -\infty} Q(x) &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} x^3 \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{x^3} - \frac{7}{x^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{5}{x^2}}\\ &= (-\infty)^3 \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{(-\infty)^3} - \frac{7}{(\infty)^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{-\infty} - \frac{5}{(-\infty)^2}}\\ &= -\infty \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{-\infty} - \frac{5}{-\infty}}{1 + \frac{2}{-\infty} - \frac{5}{+\infty}}\\ &= -\infty \cdot \frac{2 - 0 - 0}{1 + 0 - 0}\\ &= -\infty \cdot 2 = -\infty \end{align*}

This limit shows that \(Q(x)\) has left end behavior \(y \to -\infty\) as \(x \to -\infty\) (left side branch goes down).

\begin{align*} \lim_{x \to +\infty} Q(x) &= \lim_{x \to +\infty} x^3 \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{x^3} - \frac{7}{x^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{5}{x^2}}\\ &= (+\infty)^3 \cdot \frac{2 - \frac{3}{(-\infty)^3} - \frac{7}{(\infty)^5}}{1 + \frac{2}{-\infty} - \frac{5}{(-\infty)^2}}\\ &= +\infty \cdot 2 = +\infty \end{align*}

This limit shows that \(Q(x)\) has right end behavior \(y \to +\infty\) as \(x \to +\infty\) (right side branch goes up).

Note that the step where we simplified the fraction \(\frac{x^5}{x^2}\) is where the relative size of the powers of the dominant terms was important. In this case, the numerator had a higher power so that the limit ended up being infinite. When the powers are the same, this fraction cancels entirely and the other factor controls the limit. When the power in the denominator is greater, the limit of the simplified fraction will be a limit of the form \(\frac{1}{\infty}=0\text{.}\)
