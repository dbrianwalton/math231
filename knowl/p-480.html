To determine the one-sided limits, we need to test the sign analysis intervals to the left and right of \(x=-2\text{.}\) The sign analysis number line will need to have breaks at \(x=-2\) (denominator) and at \(x=1\) (numerator). So if we test using \(x=-2.1\) and \(x=-1.9\text{,}\) we get the signs on the intervals:

\begin{gather*} R(-2.1) = \frac{-2.1-1}{-2.1+2} = \frac{-3.1}{-0.1} = +31\\ R(-1.9) = \frac{-1.9-1}{-1.9+2} = \frac{-2.9}{+0.1} = -29 \end{gather*}

The sign on the left is positive; the sign on the right is negative:

\begin{gather*} \lim_{x \to -2^-} R(x) = +\infty\\ \lim_{x \to -2^+} R(x) = -\infty \end{gather*}

Because these are different, we say the two-sided limit does not exist, writing

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to -2} R(x) \; \text{d.n.e.} \end{equation*}
