The inequality is already written as a relation with zero. We need to find where the numerator equals zero (root of formula) and where the denominator equals zero (discontinuity of formula). These can be found by factoring the two polynomials. As they are both have degrees greater than 2, we will rely on computer assistance.

We can now rewrite our inequality in factored form:

\begin{equation*} \frac{x(x+2)(x-1)^2}{(x^2-2)(x+5)} \ge 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

The factors in the numerator result in zeros at \(x=0\text{,}\) \(x=-2\text{,}\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) Notice that one factor in the denominator \(x^2-2\) is still quadratic and will have two zeros. Using isolation, we find \(x=\pm\sqrt{2}\) and \(x=-5\) as the three discontinuities for our quotient.

We put these six numbers on the number line and it creates seven test intervals. Note that we need to make a distinction between the roots (making the formula equal zero) and the discontinuities (making the formula undefined).

Looking at the number line and recognizing that \(\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414\text{,}\) we might choose test points with the set \(\{-6, -3, -1.5, -1, 0.5, 1.2, 2\}\text{.}\) Notice how some of our test points are forced to be non-integers. For each of these \(x\)-values, we look at the formula

\begin{equation*} \frac{x(x+2)(x-1)^2}{(x^2-2)(x+5)} \end{equation*}

and count how many factors will be negative. Note that \((x-1)^2\) is technically two factors \((x-1)(x-1)\text{,}\) and so overall will always positive except when \(x=1\text{.}\) The table below should help you verify your thinking.

We interpret the sign analysis to answer the question. We were looking for where the formula was greater than or equal to zero. Our solution set needs to include values that make the formula's value positive (intervals) or equal to zero (points).

Notice how only some of the end-points—those where the value equals zero—are included. One of these points is a singleton isolated from the intervals. The solution set is therefore \((-5,-2] \cup (-\sqrt{2},0] \cup \{1\} \cup (\sqrt{2},\infty)\text{.}\)
