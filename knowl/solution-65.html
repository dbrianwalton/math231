Solution 5.2.4.1.

We will calculate \(f'(x)\text{,}\) which will require using the quotient rule. Then we factor the formula and complete sign analysis. Finally, we interpret the signs of \(f'(x)\) in the context of the first derivative test for monotonicity.

\begin{align*} f(x) &= \frac{x^3-2x^2}{2x+5}\\ f'(x) &= \frac{(2x+5)(3x^2-4x)-(x^3-2x^2)(2)}{(2x+5)^2}\\ &= \frac{6x^3 -8x^2+15x^2-20x-2x^3+4x^2}{(2x+5)^2}\\ &= \frac{4x^3 + 11x^2 - 20x}{(2x+5)^2}\\ &= \frac{x(4x^2+11x-20)}{(2x+5)^2}\\ &= \frac{x(4x-5)(x+4)}{(2x+5)^2} \end{align*}

Notice that we never multiplied out the denominator since that was factored already when we used the quotient rule for derivatives. The zeros of the numerator are \(x=0\text{,}\) \(x=\frac{5}{4}\text{,}\) and \(x=-4\text{,}\) while the single zero fo the denominator is \(x=-\frac{5}{2}\text{.}\) We test the signs of this factored formula for \(f'(x)\) to get a sign analysis summary shown below.

The intervals where \(f'(x)\) is negative are where \(f(x)\) is decreasing. The intervals where \(f'(x)\) is positive are where \(f(x)\) is increasing. The function \(f(x)\) is not defined at \(x=-\frac{5}{2}\) (and so certainly not continuous). When describing its shape, we will not extend the intervals to include that end point.

  • \(f(x)\) is decreasing on \((-\infty,-4]\) and on \([0,\frac{5}{4}]\text{.}\)

  • \(f(x)\) is increasing on \([-4,-\frac{5}{2})\text{,}\) on \((-\frac{5}{2},0]\) and on \([\frac{5}{4},\infty)\text{.}\)
