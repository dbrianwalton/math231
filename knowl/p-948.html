A second approach to this problem is to use the original equation involving both \(r\) and \(h\text{.}\) Differentiation this time requires the product rule and the chain rule to give

\begin{align*} \frac{dV}{dt} &= \frac{d}{dt}\left[ \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h\right]\\ &= \frac{1}{3} \pi \frac{d}{dt}\left[ r^2 \cdot h\right]\\ &= \frac{1}{3} \pi \left(\frac{d}{dt}[r^2] \cdot h + r^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dt}[h]\right)\\ &= \frac{1}{3} \pi \left(2r \frac{dr}{dt} \cdot h + r^2 \cdot \frac{dh}{dt}\right) \end{align*}

Notice that this approach introduces two additional unknowns, \(r\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\) We can find \(r\) from the similarity relation as \(r = 2\,\text{cm}\text{.}\) The similarity relation also leads to a related rates equation between \(r\) and \(h\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} 10r &= 4h\\ \frac{d}{dt}[10r] &= \frac{d}{dt}[4h]\\ 10 \frac{dr}{dt} &= 4\frac{dh}{dt}\\ \frac{dr}{dt} &= \frac{4}{10} \frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{2}{5} \frac{dh}{dt} \end{align*}

Making these substitutions and solving for \(\frac{dh}{dt}\) will result in the same answer as the first approach.
