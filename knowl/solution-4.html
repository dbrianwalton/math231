Solution 1.1.10.1.

First, we identify our variables. As in our earlier example, we will let \(M\) be the total biomass (kg) and will let \(D\) be the trunk diameter (cm). The known point is the given information \(M=38 \, \text{kg}\) and \(D=12 \, \text{cm}\text{.}\) The phrase “increased at a rate” identifies the value \(9 \, \text{kg/cm}\) as the rate of change. The word increased indicates that this is a positive value. (To say a quantity decreases would mean it has a negative rate of change.) The phrase “with respect to the diameter” lets us know that we are thinking of \(D\) as being the independent variable (\(x\)-like). This is reinforced by the units of the rate being kg per cm so that the \(y\)-variable is measured in kg and the \(x\)-variable is measured in cm.

We can immediately write down our model equation,

\begin{equation*} M-38 = 9 \cdot (D-12)\text{,} \end{equation*}

which becomes

\begin{equation*} M = 38 + 9 \cdot (D-12)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Can you see how we can view the biomass formula as \(M = 38 + \Delta M\text{?}\)

Now that we have our model equation, we can substitute the new value of \(D = 12.8 \, \text{cm}\) into our equation,

\begin{equation*} M = 38 + 9 \cdot (12.8 - 12)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Can you see how \(\Delta D = 0.8 \, \text{cm}\) is the increment of change of the diameter and how that will lead to \(\Delta M = 9 \cdot 0.8 = 7.2 \, \text{kg}\text{?}\) Consequently, our model predicts \(M = 38 + 7.2 = 45.2 \, \text{kg}\text{.}\) We predict the biomass of the tree will be 45.2 kg.
