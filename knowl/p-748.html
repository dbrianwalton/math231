Because the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}\) is true (we've not yet shown this) for any constant power \(n\text{,}\) the same process we used for polynomials to find a derivative will also work for any function that is written as a sum of constants times elementary powers of \(x\text{.}\) Polynomials require that the powers are nonnegative integers. When we have a function that is a sum of term with at least one that is a power other than a nonnegative integer (either a negative integer or a rational number), we will call our function a generalized polynomial.
