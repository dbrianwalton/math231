Solution 4.4.7.1.

As written, this function is a quotient. We can rewrite it as a composition of a power by rewriting the power in the denominator as multiplication by a negative power.

\begin{align*} f(x) &= \frac{2}{5(x^2+3x)^6}\\ &= \frac{2}{5} (x^2+3x)^{-6} \end{align*}

Our function \(y=f(x)\) is now the composition (or chain)

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y = \frac{2}{5} u^{-6} \\ u = x^2+3x \end{cases} \end{equation*}

We can compute the derivatives of each stage of the composition:

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \frac{dy}{du} = \frac{2}{5} \cdot -6 u^{-7} = \frac{-12}{5} u^{-7}\\ \frac{du}{dx} = 2x+3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

The chain rule says that the derivative of a composition (chain) is the product of the derivatives of each stage:

\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{-12}{5} u^{-7} \cdot (2x+3) \end{equation*}

We finish by substituting back in the formula for \(u\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{-12}{5(x^2+3x}^7} \cdot (2x+3) = \frac{-12(2x+3)}{5(x^2+3x)^7}\text{.} \end{equation*}
